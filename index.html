Skepticism & Conceptual Analysis

Method 01
Methodological Skepticism
Descartes suspended every belief he held until he found one that could not be doubted. The goal is constructive: by subjecting beliefs to rigorous doubt, we identify which are well-founded and which require revision. It strengthens knowledge by eliminating error.
"Could I be wrong about everything I perceive? What if my entire experience is a simulation — and the world I think I inhabit does not exist?"
Method 02
Conceptual Analysis
Rather than accepting dictionary definitions, philosophers probe concepts at their edges. Is "knowledge" just true belief, or does it require something more? Analysing these edges reveals assumptions we didn't know we were making — and exposes where theories break down.
"What exactly is justice? Is it fairness, desert, equality, or some combination of all three?"

Methods in depth · 03 & 04

Ordinary Language & Thought Experiments

Method 03
Ordinary Language Philosophy
Some philosophical problems arise from misusing everyday concepts. By examining how terms are actually used by competent speakers — not how philosophers define them in the abstract — we can dissolve puzzles that only seemed intractable. Attending to usage can reveal hidden assumptions.
"When people say 'I know it will rain,' do they mean certainty, or just strong confidence? The difference matters."
Method 04
Thought Experiments
Hypothetical scenarios strip away complexity and let us examine one feature at a time. Plato's Cave, the Trolley Problem, and Mary the Colour Scientist all work by triggering a gut reaction — then asking what that reaction reveals about our underlying commitments.
"What does your immediate response to the Trolley Problem reveal about how you really weigh outcomes and intentions?"

Reflective Equilibrium & Experimental Philosophy

Method 05
Reflective Equilibrium
Developed by John Rawls, this method moves between concrete moral judgments ("extreme inequality is unjust") and general principles ("people deserve reward for contribution"), revising both until they form a coherent whole. Neither intuitions nor theories get automatic priority — both can be wrong.
"I believe X about this case, but my principle implies not-X. Do I revise the principle, or change my judgment about the case?"
Method 06
Experimental Philosophy
If philosophical claims rest on assumptions about human intuitions, why not test those assumptions empirically? X-Phi researchers survey people across cultures to check whether philosophical intuitions are universal or culturally specific — a growing and controversial approach.
"Do people from collectivist societies think about moral responsibility differently from those in individualist ones?"

But what is philosophy

The six methods we have examined are not exercises for the classroom alone. Wherever human beings need to make decisions, define key concepts, or question assumptions — philosophy is already at work. The following slides trace where these methods appear in five domains of the real world.

Law & Justice
Philosophy in the world · Law & Justice

Defining the terms that courts decide by

"Before a judge can rule, the concepts at the centre of a case must first be defined."

Legal reasoning is applied philosophy. Every time a court decides what counts as "harm," "consent," "reasonable," or "person," it is performing conceptual analysis. Competing interpretations determine who wins and who loses — who is protected and who is not.

Legal philosophers ask foundational questions: Is there a moral obligation to obey unjust laws? What justifies punishment? Can a corporation be held morally responsible in the same way a human being can?

Example in practice
What counts as "consent"?
Debates in contract law, criminal law, and medical law all turn on what "informed consent" really means. Philosophical analysis of the concept shapes court decisions, hospital policy, and legislation worldwide.
Conceptual Analysis
Example in practice
Is civil disobedience ever justified?
Rawls used Reflective Equilibrium to argue that in a nearly just society, citizens have a moral duty to obey the law — but that this duty has limits. His framework directly shaped how courts and legislatures think about protest rights.

Medicine & Bioethics
Philosophy in the world · Medicine & Bioethics

Decisions that cannot be made by data alone

"Medicine constantly faces questions that science can inform but never fully resolve."

When does life begin or end? Who has the right to make decisions for a patient who cannot speak? Is it ethical to prioritise younger patients when resources are scarce? These are not technical questions — they are philosophical ones.

Bioethicists use thought experiments (the trolley-problem structure appears constantly in triage ethics), conceptual analysis (what exactly is "death"?), and experimental philosophy (cross-cultural studies on euthanasia intuitions).

Example in practice
When is a patient truly "informed"?
The legal and medical concept of informed consent rests on philosophical accounts of autonomy, rationality, and understanding. Different analyses yield different protocols — and different ethical conclusions about whose voice counts.
Conceptual Analysis
Example in practice
The ventilator dilemma
During COVID-19, hospitals worldwide faced impossible triage choices. Medical ethicists constructed thought experiments to test allocation principles — life-years, age, lottery — and mapped how our intuitions conflicted with our stated values.

AI & Technology
Philosophy in the world · AI & Technology

The newest machines raise the oldest questions

"Can a system that processes language be said to understand it? Should we care if it suffers?"

Artificial intelligence has forced philosophy back into the mainstream. Questions once thought purely theoretical — about consciousness, personhood, responsibility, and fairness — now carry billion-dollar consequences.

Tech companies and governments actively recruit philosophers. The EU AI Act, debates about autonomous weapons, and algorithmic bias lawsuits are all, at their core, philosophical disputes about concepts like "harm," "agency," and "fairness."

Example in practice
Can a machine be responsible for a decision?
When an AI system recommends a prison sentence or denies a loan, conceptual analysis of "responsibility" and "moral agency" determines whether the system, its designers, or its users should be held accountable — and to whom.
Conceptual Analysis
Example in practice
Does an AI have interests worth protecting?
Experimental philosophers are now surveying people about their moral intuitions toward AI systems — whether chatbots "seem" to suffer, and whether that seeming matters. These studies directly inform AI ethics guidelines and policy.
Experimental Philosophy
Business & Leadership
Philosophy in the world · Business & Leadership

What does a company owe the world?

"Before you can act ethically as a leader, you need to know what 'ethical' means in your context."

Business ethics is not a soft add-on to "real" business. Corporate decisions about labour practices, environmental impact, and executive pay all rest on implicit philosophical claims about fairness, responsibility, and what organisations owe to stakeholders.

Leaders who have not examined these assumptions often act on contradictory principles without realising it — precisely the situation Reflective Equilibrium is designed to resolve.

Example in practice
Shareholder vs. stakeholder models
Friedman's "profit only" doctrine and Freeman's stakeholder theory represent competing philosophical positions about the purpose of a corporation. Ordinary language analysis of "responsibility" reveals that most people's intuitions don't fit either model cleanly.
Ordinary Language Philosophy
Example in practice
Questioning your own assumptions
The most effective leadership frameworks draw directly on methodological skepticism: systematically question which assumptions about your market, customers, or culture would survive scrutiny — and which are myths your organisation has simply stopped examining.

Everyday Life
Philosophy in the world · Everyday Life

The examined life as a practical skill

"Philosophical method is not just what academics do — it is what careful thinkers do."

Every time you question whether a belief is well-founded, you practise methodological skepticism. Every time you notice a word used inconsistently in an argument, you do ordinary language philosophy. Every time you think "but what if the situation were slightly different?", you construct a thought experiment.

Philosophical thinking helps us make more consistent decisions, spot manipulation in public discourse, and understand our own values more clearly.

Example in practice
Personal identity and big decisions
Am I the same person I was ten years ago? If not, what gives me continuity across time? These questions matter when thinking about long-term commitments, regret, and personal responsibility. Thought experiments about gradual change help us test our intuitions.
Thought Experiments
Example in practice
Holding consistent values
Reflective Equilibrium is the method behind living by your principles. When a concrete decision contradicts a value you claim to hold, you face a choice: revise the value, or revise the decision. Examining that tension honestly is exactly what Rawls described.
